Recent news about South Africa

South Africa’s economy under pressure after recent credit rating cuts to junk by major rating agencies.

Introduction

It is well known that South Africa’s economy has not been going too well in the recent years. Political instability, socio-economic issues and slow economic growth – a downgrading was long due. Even though predictions for this year’s GDP went slightly up to 1% due to a moderate rebound in commodity prices and a comeback in agricultural production (corn-production is expected to be the highest since 1981), the country could now face a further downgrading by rating agencies. South African ministers met with S&P Global Ratings officials on Monday, 22/05/2017, as they want to avoid another downgrade. S&P will make another rating announcement on June 2, while also Moody’s has placed the country under review for a downgrade.

Political

Increased political risk which will remain elevated this year due to controversy surrounding President Jacob Zuma and the ANC. A cabinet reshuffle in which 10 of 35 ministers were fired is the main cause of downgrades from Moody’s, S&P and Fitch. Zuma replaced Finance minister, Pravin Gordhan, who was considered a bulwark2 against government corruption. The new ministers, are largely unknown to investors which could create further uncertainty in SA’s financial markets. The reshuffle was done without ANC’s approval. Therefore, internal government and party divisions could delay fiscal and structural reforms. Overall: Increased likelihood that economic growth and fiscal outcomes could suffer.3 Political uncertainty in SA is a consequence of low growth for social stability and suffering industrial relations, especially in the mining sector.

Economy

Recent developments

Medium-term growth prospects are poor due to structural weaknesses, including continuing energy shortages, rising interest rates, further capital outflows and less supportive capital market environment. In addition, low consumer and business confidence 4has been putting further pressure on growth of economic activity, however there is a noticeable, slight upward trend. Slow progress in delivering economic and social services in townships and rural areas, hence government is behind their expectations with their key government policies. Real GDP growth declined each year from 2013 until 2016, from 2.48% in 2013 to 0.28% in 2016. Real economic growth turned negative in 2016Q4. Main contributor to the economy’s slowdown is an 11.5% drop in the mining’s sector production.

Unemployment has risen from 2013 until 2016, from respectively 24.73% up to 27.1% in 2016Q4. An increasing Debt-To-GDP Ratio from 47.1% in 2014 to 52% in 2016 is one indicator of the low-growth environment. Following the negative economic environment, South Africa’s credit rating has recently been downgraded to junk status by all major rating agencies. Standard & Poor’s and Fitch rate SA now with BB+ whereas Moody’s downgraded the debt rating to Baa2 from Baa1. South African 10 year bond yields have been falling since the beginning of the year, however this caused the bond yields to a new high in 2017 and a further weakening of the rand. Since that high at 9.22% on 10th of April the 10 year bond yield fell to 8.62%. A weak rand is pushing up inflation which increases pressure on SA’s Reserve Bank. Inflation in 2016 was at 6.3% above the central bank’s target of 3-6%.

Short to medium-term outlook

Fiscal policy is now under pressure from the recent ratings downgrades. Continued increase in government debt, higher borrowing rates, low growth and high inflation provide a weak economic environment which has led to further capital outflow in the past weeks. A weaker rand also means higher import prices for consumers, even though it might further boost tourism in South Africa. Under the given conditions it seems like that the economy is not likely to increase the pace of recovery and it is unlikely to prevent a further increase in unemployment. The strong institutional framework built on accountability and transparency which the South African treasury claims as one of its strengths, can in fact only be seen when looking at the central bank and exactly this puts pressure on president Zuma. SA’s exports account for about 30% of its GDP and as China’s growth is slowing down (China is the single-largest export destinations for SA), so are South Africa’s exports. This is due to an appreciation of the USD and a devaluation of the RMB, so both the currencies highly effect SA’s trade and should be looked at closely. In addition, South Africa will need to refinance about $18b maturing bonds this year – about 12% of its total issuance - according to Reuters data. This could prove costly for South Africa with further increasing bond yields.

Central Bank

The MPC decided in January to keep the interest rate unchanged at 7%. The inflation outlook would have improved (due to a further appreciation of the rand) according to the MPC meeting from 30th of March. This outlook might be in need of adjustment due to recent credit rating downgrades leading to a stop in the appreciation of the rand. As anticipated in their last meeting, political uncertainty has weakened the outlook for the rand. Overall, as indicated in last S&P report, the SARB has a track record in achieving price stability, flexibility in its monetary policy remains and is considered as independently operating. The exchange rate regime in South Africa is a floating one, i.e. a non-targeted interest rate. Next MPC Meetings: 23-25 May, 18-20 July, 19-21 September

Financial Markets

South Africa’s most important stock index, the FTSE/JSE (JALSH) is down from 54,666 to 54,453 (As of 22th of May 4:45 pm) index points (ZAR). Due to a deterioration in sovereign creditworthiness, risks to the banking sector increase. This is likely to negatively affect bank’s financial metrics, and therefore knock-on effects on profitability and capital. S&P already had downgraded the banks Nedbank, Absa, Investec and FirstRand to non-investment grade BB+ on 6th of April to fall in line with the country’s credit rating. Johannesburg's Banks Index had subsequently lost 10 , but came back and is now around 6% lower since March 27.

Credit Ratings

  • S&P: BB + (April 2017) and negative Outlook
  • Moody’s: Baa2 (April 2017) and stable Outlook
  • Fitch: BB+ (April 2017) and stable outlook

Conclusion

The country could now drop out of some widely used global bond index which rely on investment grades and consequently $15b in investment funds are at risk to flow out of South Africa6. If South Africa cannot maintain the remaining investment-grade rating of local-currency debt, then rand-denominated bonds will be excluded of those indices. Subsequent capital outflows and increasing borrowing costs, would add further pressure on the rand.

Investors now will have to look at the new rating announcements by Moody’s and S&P. South Africa’s Central Bank has limited room for stimulus through macroeconomic policies and policy rates are expected to remain on hold since headline inflation is expected to return to 5.9% in 2017 which is below the 6% target. Thus, the priority to stimulate economic growth and job creation can only be achieved through fiscal stimulus: structural reforms, above all in product and service markets and in the labour market.

Current economic prospects for South Africa are poor. Low expectations for economic growth, at a rate lower than population growth, within the next 2 years and ongoing political uncertainties will be main issues investors will face when making investment decisions in South Africa.
